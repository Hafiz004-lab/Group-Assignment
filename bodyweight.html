How to Practice Bodyweight

12 january 2025 12 comments Aiman

A bodyweight workout is a form of exercise that uses your body as resistance instead of external weights or equipment. It is highly effective for building strength, endurance, flexibility, and balance while being convenient and accessible, as it can be done anywhere.

1. Set Clear Goals

These workouts are scalable for all fitness levels, with variations and progressions to increase difficulty over time. They’re versatile, can be done anywhere, and often involve circuits or intervals to combine strength and cardio for a full-body challenge. With proper form, a warm-up, and a cool-down, bodyweight workouts are an effective and accessible way to achieve fitness goals.

Determine your fitness objectives: strength, endurance, flexibility, or a mix.



2. Plan Your Workout
Planning a bodyweight workout involves several key steps to ensure it's effective, tailored to your goals, and safe.

a. Define Your Fitness Goals

To define bodyweight fitness goals, decide what you want to achieve: strength (e.g., master push-ups or pull-ups), endurance (e.g., longer planks or high-rep exercises), fat loss (e.g., HIIT circuits), or flexibility (e.g., improve mobility and range of motion). Make goals specific, measurable, and time-bound, such as "Do 20 push-ups in 4 weeks." Adjust based on your current fitness level and progress.


b. Divide Your Workout into Phases

A bodyweight workout has three phases. Begin with a 5–10 minute warm-up of dynamic stretches and light cardio to prepare your body. Follow with a 20–30 minute main workout, including exercises for the upper body, lower body, core, and full-body movements, done in circuits or sets. Finish with a 5–10 minute cool-down of static stretches to improve flexibility and aid recovery.


c. Schedule Your Rest Periods.

Rest periods should be 30–60 seconds between exercises in circuits and 1–2 minutes between rounds or sets. Include 1–2 rest days per week for recovery, adjusting rest times based on your fitness level and goals.


d. Track Your Progress

There are various ways of tracking your progress. One example is to monitor reps, sets, or time for each exercise. You could also adjust intensity weekly by increasing reps, rounds, or trying advanced variations once you feel confident enough.



3. Focus on Form
Bodyweight workout form is crucial to prevent injury, maximize effectiveness, and ensure proper muscle engagement. Correct form ensures you're targeting the right muscles, reduces strain on joints, and improves long-term progress. Poor form can lead to ineffective results and increase the risk of injury, so it's important to prioritize technique over speed or reps.

Here are a few examples of bodyweight workout form:

Push-up: Keep your body in a straight line from head to heels, engage your core, and lower your chest to the floor without flaring elbows too wide.

Squat: Keep your chest upright, knees behind toes, and squat down as if sitting in a chair, ensuring your thighs are parallel to the ground.

Plank: Maintain a straight line from head to heels, avoid sagging hips, and engage your core to prevent lower back strain.
